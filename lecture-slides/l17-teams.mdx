## Cognitive Diversity Produces Better Software

<p style={{fontSize: '0.85em'}}>
Having members who think differently isn't just nice—it's <strong>productive</strong>.
</p>

<ul style={{fontSize: '0.8em'}}>
  <li><strong>Different perspectives catch different blind spots.</strong> A security-focused engineer notices vulnerabilities a feature-focused one misses.</li>
  <li><strong>Constructive disagreement improves decisions.</strong> When everyone thinks alike, bad ideas go unchallenged.</li>
  <li><strong>Creativity emerges from intersection.</strong> New ideas often come from combining insights across domains.</li>
</ul>

<p style={{fontSize: '0.85em', marginTop: '1em', fontWeight: 'bold', color: '#9370DB'}}>
The discomfort of having your assumptions challenged is the feeling of blind spots being revealed.
</p>

<aside className="notes">
**Key insight:**
- Cognitive diversity = different thinking styles, backgrounds, approaches
- It's not just about demographics—it's about how people solve problems
- Working with people different from you produces better outcomes

> **Transition:** But teams come with a cost...
</aside>

<aside className="notes">
**Brooks' Law (1975, *The Mythical Man-Month*):**
- Adding people to a late project makes it later
- New members need onboarding
- More people = more coordination overhead
- n(n-1)/2 communication paths

**Implication:** Teams need to plan how they communicate upfront. You can't just throw more people at a problem.

## Scalable Knowledge Sharing

<div style={{display: 'grid', gridTemplateColumns: '1fr 1fr 1fr', gap: '0.5em', fontSize: '0.7em'}}>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(255,100,100,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**One-to-one** (doesn't scale)

- Mentorship sessions
- Hallway conversations
- Direct messages

</div>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(255,200,100,0.15)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**One-to-many** (scales better)

- Code review
- Pair programming
- Q&A forums

</div>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(100,255,100,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**One-to-many, persistent** (scales best)

- Documentation & wikis
- Recorded tech talks
- Well-commented code

</div>

</div>

<p style={{fontSize: '0.85em', marginTop: '1em', fontWeight: 'bold', color: '#9370DB'}}>
Rule of thumb: If you've explained something to more than two people, write it down.
</p>

<p style={{fontSize: '0.75em', color: '#666'}}>
Research shows developers see ~50% productivity boost when documentation is up-to-date, detailed, and available in multiple formats.
</p>

<aside className="notes">
**The key insight:**
- Brooks' Law means we need communication that scales sub-linearly
- One-to-one conversations are valuable but don't scale
- Persistent, searchable documentation is the best investment

**The third explanation should be a link.**

## Tuckman's Stages of Team Formation

<Img
  src="/img/lectures/web/l17-tuckman-stages.webp"
  prompt="An illustration showing Tuckman's four stages of team formation as a journey or path that a team walks along together. Show the team progressing through four distinct zones:

One person points at a whiteboard saying 'My way!', another crosses arms saying 'That won't work!', a third looks frustrated. The ground is cracking beneath them. STAGE 3 - NORMING (yellow/warm, calming): The storm is clearing. Team members sit around a table with a document titled 'Team Agreement'. They're nodding, shaking hands, establishing rules. A rainbow appears. Thought bubbles: 'OK, let's agree on...', 'I see your point.'

  alt="Four stages of team formation as a journey: Forming (blue, polite strangers with name tags), Storming (red, arguing with lightning bolts, marked NECESSARY), Norming (yellow, team around agreement document with rainbow), Performing (green, team working smoothly as meshing gears with star/trophy)."
/>

<aside className="notes">
**Tuckman's model (psychologist Bruce Tuckman):**
1. **Forming:** Polite, guarded, everyone's on best behavior
2. **Storming:** Conflicts emerge—different styles clash. Uncomfortable but NECESSARY
3. **Norming:** Shared expectations form, trust develops
4. **Performing:** Team operates effectively, members compensate for each other

**Key insight:** Conflict (storming) is normal, not a sign of failure. The goal is to move through it productively.

> **Transition:** How do teams move from storming to norming faster?
</aside>

## Team Contracts Accelerate Norming

<p style={{fontSize: '0.85em'}}>
Explicitly discuss and document expectations <strong>before</strong> conflicts arise:
</p>

<div style={{display: 'grid', gridTemplateColumns: '1fr 1fr', gap: '1em', fontSize: '0.75em'}}>

<div>

- When are we available to work together?
- How do we prefer to communicate?
- How do we handle deadlines?

</div>

<div>

- What does "done" mean for our work?
- How will we divide work and make decisions?
- What do we do when someone is blocked?

</div>

</div>

<div className="fragment" style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(147, 112, 219, 0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px', fontSize: '0.8em', marginTop: '0.5em'}}>

**Example conflict prevented:** A team member who starts work early and one who works best under deadline pressure can negotiate expectations upfront—rather than discovering the mismatch mid-project.

</div>

<aside className="notes">
**Team contracts:**
- Surface potential conflicts before they become problems
- Create a reference document to return to during disagreements
- Not a legal document—a shared understanding

**This matters for your group projects:** You'll establish team contracts in CYB10.

> **Transition:** Once the team is norming, how do we track progress?

<p style={{fontSize: '0.75em', marginTop: '0.5em'}}>
From <em>Team Geek</em> / <em>Debugging Teams</em> by Fitzpatrick & Collins-Sussman (former Google engineers).

<aside className="notes">
**HRT (pronounced "heart"):**
- **Humility:** You're not omniscient or infallible
- **Respect:** You genuinely care about your teammates
- **Trust:** You believe others are competent

**These are pillars we will hold you accountable to in group work in this course.**

<Slide>

## HRT in Practice

<div style={{display: 'grid', gridTemplateColumns: '1fr 1fr 1fr', gap: '0.5em', fontSize: '0.65em'}}>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(100,150,255,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**Humility**

- Admit when you don't know something
- Accept that your code can be improved
- Be willing to change your mind
- Recognize others' valuable perspectives

</div>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(100,255,100,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**Respect**

- Give credit where it's due
- Listen to understand, not just to respond
- Assume competence and good intentions
- Value contributions different from yours

</div>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(147,112,219,0.15)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**Trust**

- Delegate without micromanaging
- Give space to make and learn from mistakes
- Don't rewrite others' code without discussion
- Trust teammates to ask for help when needed

</div>

</div>

<aside className="notes">
**Each pillar is actionable:**
- Humility isn't self-deprecation—it's honest self-assessment
- Respect isn't just politeness—it's genuine care
- Trust isn't blind—it's giving people the benefit of the doubt

**The test:** Think about your best team experience. Which pillars were present?

> **Transition:** Where HRT is tested every day...
</aside>

## Applying HRT to Code Review

<div style={{display: 'grid', gridTemplateColumns: '1fr 1fr', gap: '1em', fontSize: '0.75em'}}>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(255,100,100,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**Without HRT**

> "This is wrong. Use a HashMap instead."

> "Why would you do it this way?"

> "This whole function needs to be rewritten."

</div>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(100,255,100,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**With HRT**

> "Have you considered using a HashMap here? It would give us O(1) lookups instead of O(n)."

> "I'm curious about this approach—what led you to this design?"

> "I'm having trouble following the logic in this function. Could we pair on simplifying it?"

</div>

</div>

<p className="fragment" style={{fontSize: '0.8em', marginTop: '0.5em', color: '#9370DB'}}>
Same technical goals. The HRT version treats the author as a capable colleague, not a subordinate to be corrected.
</p>

<aside className="notes">
**Key pattern:**
- Ask questions rather than making demands
- Explain your reasoning
- Acknowledge good work
- Critique the code, not the person

**Both versions achieve the same technical outcome—but one builds the team, the other erodes it.**

> **Transition:** Even with HRT, conflicts still happen...

## Conflict Resolution: Six Steps

<div style={{fontSize: '0.75em'}}>

| Step | Action | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1 | **Establish shared facts** | "The build broke at 11 PM. Here's the commit." |
| 2 | **Express feelings, not accusations** | "I felt stressed when..." not "You always..." |
| 3 | **Never presume intent** | Assume good faith until proven otherwise |
| 4 | **Remain calm** | If triggered, step away and return later |
| 5 | **Fight forward, not back** | Focus on solutions, not blame |
| 6 | **Escalate appropriately** | Bring in a neutral third party if stuck |

</div>

<aside className="notes">
**Key principle:** "Fight forward, not back"
- Blame is backward-looking and unproductive
- Solutions are forward-looking
- "How do we prevent this next time?" > "Whose fault was this?"

**Escalation isn't failure:** Sometimes you need a neutral third party. That's mature, not weak.

> **Transition:** HRT also means being someone your team can rely on...
</aside>

## Accountability: Being Someone Your Team Can Rely On

<ul style={{fontSize: '0.8em'}}>
  <li><strong>Own your commitments:</strong> If you say you'll do something, do it—or communicate early if you can't. Don't wait until the deadline.</li>
  <li className="fragment"><strong>Take responsibility for outcomes, not just effort:</strong> "I worked hard on it" isn't the same as "it's done and working."</li>
  <li className="fragment"><strong>Support team outcomes:</strong> If a teammate is struggling, offer help before being asked. The team succeeds or fails together.</li>
</ul>

<p className="fragment" style={{fontSize: '0.85em', marginTop: '1em', color: '#9370DB'}}>
Accountability builds trust. When teammates can count on you, the whole team moves faster.
</p>

<aside className="notes">
**Accountability isn't blame—it's reliability.**
- Struggling silently isn't heroic—ask for help
- When you make a mistake, acknowledge it and focus on the fix
- If you finish early, ask what else needs doing

## Seeking Feedback Is a Professional Skill

<div style={{display: 'grid', gridTemplateColumns: '1fr 1fr', gap: '1em', fontSize: '0.75em'}}>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(255,100,100,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**Vague (unhelpful)**

"What did you think of my code?"

</div>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(100,255,100,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**Specific (actionable)**

"I'm trying to improve my API design. Were the method signatures in this PR clear? What would have made them clearer?"

</div>

</div>

<div style={{fontSize: '0.8em', marginTop: '1em'}}>

**How to seek feedback effectively:**
- **Ask specific questions** about areas you want to improve
- **Ask the right people** — someone honest, with relevant expertise
- **Make it safe to be honest** — "Honest feedback helps more than kind feedback"

</div>

<aside className="notes">
**Why this matters:**
- Others see things about your work that you can't
- Waiting for feedback to come to you = slow growth
- Proactively seeking uncomfortable feedback is what separates rapid growers from those who plateau

> **Transition:** Receiving feedback is the harder half...
</aside>

</Slide>

<Slide>

## Receiving Feedback Without Defensiveness

<p style={{fontSize: '0.85em'}}>
Your instinct may be to explain, defend, or minimize. Resist.
</p>

<ol style={{fontSize: '0.8em'}}>
  <li><strong>Say "thank you"</strong> before responding — it creates space between reaction and response</li>
  <li><strong>Ask clarifying questions</strong> — "Can you give me an example?"</li>
  <li><strong>Reflect before reacting</strong> — you don't have to agree immediately, but don't dismiss</li>
  <li><strong>Follow up</strong> — tell them what you changed. It encourages future feedback.</li>
</ol>

<p className="fragment" style={{fontSize: '0.85em', marginTop: '1em', fontWeight: 'bold', color: '#9370DB'}}>
The discomfort is the point. If all feedback feels good, you're only hearing from people who tell you what you want to hear.
</p>

<aside className="notes">
**The feedback paradox:**
- The most useful feedback is the most uncomfortable
- It reveals blind spots you didn't know you had
- Seek out people who will challenge you

## Anatomy of a Good GitHub Issue

<div style={{display: 'grid', gridTemplateColumns: '1fr 1fr', gap: '1em', fontSize: '0.7em'}}>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(255,100,100,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**Bad issue**

**Title:** "Unable to login"

**Body:** "It doesn't work. Please fix."

</div>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(100,255,100,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**Good issue**

**Title:** "Login fails silently when password contains special characters"

**Body:**
1. Go to login page
2. Enter username "testuser"
3. Enter password "p@ss#word!"
4. Click "Sign In"
5. Nothing happens, no error displayed

**Expected:** Error message or successful login
**Environment:** Chrome 120, macOS 14.2

</div>

</div>

<aside className="notes">
**A good issue includes:**
- Clear, specific title
- Context: what you were trying to do
- Reproduction steps (for bugs)
- Expected vs. actual behavior
- Environment details
- Screenshots (for UI issues)

**Caveat:** If you're not certain about the root cause, don't assume—"Unable to login" may be better than guessing wrong.

> **Transition:** Similar principles apply to pull requests...
</aside>

</Slide>

<Slide>

## Pull Request Best Practices

<div style={{fontSize: '0.8em'}}>

**Link to the issue:**

```markdown
Fixes #42

This PR adds input validation for special characters in passwords.
```

</div>

<div style={{display: 'grid', gridTemplateColumns: '1fr 1fr', gap: '1em', fontSize: '0.7em', marginTop: '0.5em'}}>

<div>

**Keep changes small and focused**
- One logical change per PR
- Reviewable in 15-30 minutes
- Easier to review, easier to revert

</div>

<div>

**Meaningful commit messages**

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(255,100,100,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px', fontSize: '0.8em'}}>

This question received downvotes and no helpful answers. Why?

<ul style={{fontSize: '0.75em'}}>
  <li>Vague title that doesn't describe the problem</li>
  <li>"Can't seem to get it" — what's going wrong?</li>
  <li>No error message or unexpected behavior described</li>
  <li>No indication of what was already tried</li>
</ul>

<aside className="notes">
**This is a real Stack Overflow question.**
- It wastes everyone's time, including the asker's
- People want to help, but they need information to do so

## Make It Easy for People to Help You

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(147, 112, 219, 0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px', fontSize: '0.75em'}}>

> **I am trying to** [goal] **so that I can** [context/motivation].
>
> **I am running into** [specific problem—error message, unexpected behavior].
>
> **I have looked at** [resources consulted] **and tried** [approaches attempted].
>
> **My tech stack is:** [relevant versions and configuration].
>
> **I think the problem could be** [your hypothesis, if you have one].

</div>

<div className="fragment" style={{fontSize: '0.8em', marginTop: '0.5em'}}>

**Before asking, search first:**
- Stack Overflow, team Slack/Discord history, project docs, closed GitHub issues

**After solving, document the solution:**
- Update the original thread, add to wiki, write a blog post

</div>

<aside className="notes">
**Why this template works:**
- Goal and context help others understand what you actually need
- Specific problem lets people diagnose without guessing
- Resources consulted shows you've done your homework
- Your hypothesis gives experts a starting point

**This connects back to scalable knowledge sharing:** Every documented solution is one less interruption for your teammates.

</aside>

## Key Takeaways

<ul style={{fontSize: '0.8em'}}>
  <li><strong>Teams beat individuals</strong> because nobody is an expert in everything. Cognitive diversity produces better software.</li>
  <li><strong>Communication overhead</strong> grows quadratically—invest in scalable knowledge sharing (documentation, code review).</li>
  <li><strong>Storming is normal.</strong> Team contracts and explicit agreements accelerate norming.</li>
  <li><strong>HRT (Humility, Respect, Trust)</strong> is the foundation of effective collaboration. We hold you accountable to these.</li>
  <li><strong>Seek feedback proactively</strong>—the discomfort is the point.</li>
  <li><strong>Write clearly:</strong> good issues, focused PRs, specific questions. Make it easy for others to help you.</li>
</ul>

<aside className="notes">
**For your group projects:**
- Establish team contracts early
- Use GitHub Issues and Projects to track work
- Apply HRT in every interaction
- Seek and give honest feedback
- Write clear issues, PRs, and questions

**The skills in this lecture are career skills.** Technical ability gets you hired. Collaboration skills determine your trajectory.
