### Communication Overhead Grows Quadratically

In a team of *n* people, the number of communication paths is:

$$\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$$

| Team Size | Communication Paths |
|-----------|---------------------|
| 2         | 1                   |
| 4         | 6                   |
| 8         | 28                  |
| 16        | 120                 |

Communication overhead grows faster than team productivity.

Amazon has the two-pizza rule: Teams should be small enough (5-8 people) to be fed by
two large pizzas.

<aside className="notes">
- This is why Brooks' Law holds: new team members need to be brought up to speed
- Everyone spends more time coordinating and less time coding
- Important to plan upfront how to structure a team
→ This is why we need communication mechanisms that scale better
</aside>

</Slide>

<Slide>

### Scalable Knowledge Sharing

<div className='fragment'>
**One-to-one** (doesn't scale):
- Mentorship sessions
- Hallway conversations
- Pair programming
- Direct messages
</div>

<div className='fragment'>
**One-to-many** (scales better):
- Code review (synchronous, but knowledge spreads)
- Q&A forums and mailing lists (asynchronous, searchable)
</div>

<div className='fragment'>
**One-to-many, persistent** (scales best):
- Documentation and wikis
- Recorded tech talks
- Blog posts and tutorials
- Well-commented code
</div>
<aside className="notes">
- The key is choosing communication methods that don't grow quadratically with team size
- Persistent methods are best because they can be referenced repeatedly
→ So how do we apply this principle?
</aside>

</Slide>

<Slide>

### Write It Down

Developers see approximately a **50% productivity boost** when documentation is up-to-date, detailed, and available in multiple formats.

**Tips:**
- If you've explained something to more than two people, write it down. The third explanation should be a link to documentation.
- Fix out-of-date documentation as you work through it.

Good documentation isn't just nice to have—it's a force multiplier for the entire team.

<aside className="notes">
- Documentation reduces interruptions and scales sub-linearly with team growth
- Every documented solution is one less interruption for your teammates
- That future team member might be you in six months
- Transition: Beyond knowledge sharing, teams need to work through stages of development...
</aside>

</Slide>

### Team Contracts

When forming a new team, explicitly discuss and document expectations:

<div className="fragment">

**Availability & Communication:**
- When are we available to work together?
- How do we prefer to communicate? (Text, video call, in-person?)

</div>

<div className="fragment">

**Work Style:**
- How do we handle deadlines? (Head start vs. last-minute?)
- What does "done" mean for our work?

</div>

<div className="fragment">

**Process & Support:**
- How will we divide work and make decisions?
- What do we do when someone is blocked or struggling?
- What tools should we use to organize information?

</div>

<aside className="notes">
- These conversations surface potential conflicts BEFORE they become problems
- Example: Early starter vs deadline worker can negotiate upfront
- Better to discover mismatches at week 1 than mid-project
- Transition: GitHub provides tools to track this work
</aside>

### The HRT Framework

**HRT** (pronounced "heart") are the three pillars of effective team collaboration:

<div className="fragment">

* **Humility**: You are not the center of the universe (nor is your code!). You're open to self-improvement.

</div>

<div className="fragment">

* **Respect**: You genuinely care about others you work with. You treat them kindly and appreciate their abilities.

</div>

<div className="fragment">

* **Trust**: You believe others are competent and will do the right thing. You're OK with letting them drive when appropriate.

</div>

<br/><br/><br/>
<div style={{ fontSize: '.8em' }}>
Source: [*Team Geek*](https://learning.oreilly.com/library/view/team-geek/9781449329839/) (later [*Debugging Teams*](https://www.debuggingteams.com/)) by Brian Fitzpatrick and Ben Collins-Sussman,
former Google engineers.
</div>

<aside className="notes">
- These are pillars we will hold you accountable to in group work
- Transition: Let's look at what each means in practice
</aside>

</Slide>

<Slide>

### Humility in Practice

You are not the center of the universe (nor is your code!). You're neither omniscient nor infallible. You're open to self-improvement.

**This means:**
- Admitting when you don't know something
- Accepting that your code can be improved
- Being willing to change your mind when presented with better arguments
- Recognizing that others have valuable perspectives

It does not mean being a doormat.

<aside className="notes">
- Opposite: "I'm the expert, my way is best"
- Humility doesn't mean lacking confidence in your abilities
- It means being open to learning and improvement
</aside>

</Slide>

<Slide>

### Respect in Practice

You genuinely care about others you work with. You treat them kindly and appreciate their abilities and accomplishments.

**This means:**
- Giving credit where it's due
- Listening to understand, not just to respond
- Assuming competence and good intentions
- Valuing contributions different from your own

<aside className="notes">
- Opposite: Dismissing others' ideas, taking credit for joint work
- Respect is shown through actions, not just words
</aside>

</Slide>

<Slide>

### Trust in Practice

You believe others are competent and will do the right thing, and you're OK with letting them drive when appropriate.

**This means:**
- Delegating without micromanaging
- Giving people space to make (and learn from) mistakes
- Not rewriting others' code without discussion
- Trusting teammates to ask for help when needed

<aside className="notes">
- Opposite: Micromanaging, redoing others' work without asking
- Trust is built over time through consistent behavior
- Transition: Let's see HRT in action during code review
</aside>

</Slide>

### HRT in Code Review

<div style={{display: 'grid', gridTemplateColumns: '1fr 1fr', gap: '1em', fontSize: '0.7em'}}>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(255,100,100,0.1)', padding: '0.5em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**Without HRT ❌**

"This is wrong. Use a HashMap instead."<br/><br/>

"Have you considered using a HashMap here? It would give us O(1) lookups instead of O(n)."

### When Conflicts Arise

Even with HRT, conflicts happen. When they do:

1. **Work from the same set of facts** - Establish what everyone actually knows
2. **Talk about feelings** - "I felt frustrated when..." vs. "You always..."
3. **Never presume intent** - Assume good faith until proven otherwise
4. **Remain calm** - Step away if triggered, return later
5. **Fight forward, not back** - Focus on solutions, not blame
6. **Escalate appropriately** - Bring in neutral third party if needed
7. **Talk face-to-face.** Misunderstandings can snowball online

<aside className="notes">
- Conflicts often stem from different assumptions or information
- Decisions made in anger rarely improve the situation
- When escalating, don't expect others to solve it for you
- Transition: Beyond handling conflict, teams need accountability
</aside>

</Slide>

### My Experience: The five stages of dealing with a code review

<ol>
<li className='fragment'>Disbelief: "How could someone find so much to complain about in my
code?!"</li>

<li className='fragment'>Anger: "How dare someone suggest that a hash-map of hash-maps
isn't an efficient way of storing data?"</li>

<li className='fragment'>Bargaining: "Maybe if I add the comments the reviewer requested in
one file, I won't have to fix the tricky Unicode issues in another."</li>

<li className='fragment'>Depression: "There was so much wrong with my code. How can I
show my face to the person who reviewed my code?"</li>

<li className='fragment'>Acceptance: "Wow, my code is a lot better due to the feedback. This
is a useful process, although I bet it's occasionally painful for
everyone."</li>
</ol>

### Issues

**Issues** are the fundamental unit of work tracking:

- Each issue represents a bug, feature, or task
- Issues can be assigned to team members
- Labels help categorize and filter (bug, enhancement, documentation, etc.)
- Milestones group issues into releases or sprints

### Commits

* Each commit should ideally be a logical set of changes

* Commit messages should be meaningful
   - ❌ "fix bug"
   - ❌ "WIP"
   - ✅ "Add regex validation for password special characters"
   - ✅ "Display error toast when login validation fails"

* First line should be no more than 50 characters
