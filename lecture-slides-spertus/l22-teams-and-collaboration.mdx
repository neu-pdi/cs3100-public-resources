## Communication Overhead Grows Quadratically

In a team of *n* people, the number of communication paths is:

$$\frac{n(n-1)}{2}$$

| Team Size | Communication Paths |
|-----------|---------------------|
| 2         | 1                   |
| 4         | 6                   |
| 8         | 28                  |
| 16        | 120                 |

Communication overhead grows faster than team productivity.

Amazon has the two-pizza rule: Teams should be small enough (5-8 people) to be fed by
two large pizzas.

<aside className="notes">
- This is why Brooks' Law holds: new team members need to be brought up to speed
- Everyone spends more time coordinating and less time coding
- Important to plan upfront how to structure a team
→ This is why we need communication mechanisms that scale better
</aside>

</Slide>

## Scalable Knowledge Sharing

<div className='fragment'>
**One-to-one** (doesn't scale):
- Mentorship sessions
- Hallway conversations
- Pair programming
- Direct messages
</div>

<div className='fragment'>
**One-to-many** (scales better):
- Code review (synchronous, but knowledge spreads)
- Q&A forums and mailing lists (asynchronous, searchable)
</div>

<div className='fragment'>
**One-to-many, persistent** (scales best):
- Documentation and wikis
- Recorded tech talks
- Blog posts and tutorials
- Well-commented code
</div>
<aside className="notes">
- The key is choosing communication methods that don't grow quadratically with team size
- Persistent methods are best because they can be referenced repeatedly
→ So how do we apply this principle?
</aside>

## Write It Down

Developers see approximately a **50% productivity boost** when documentation is up-to-date, detailed, and available in multiple formats.

**Tips:**
- If you've explained something to more than two people, write it down. The third explanation should be a link to documentation.
- Fix out-of-date documentation as you work through it.

Good documentation isn't just nice to have—it's a force multiplier for the entire team.

<aside className="notes">
- Documentation reduces interruptions and scales sub-linearly with team growth
- Every documented solution is one less interruption for your teammates
- That future team member might be you in six months
- Transition: Beyond knowledge sharing, teams need to work through stages of development...
</aside>

## Team Contracts

When forming a new team, explicitly discuss and document expectations:

<div className="fragment">

**Availability & Communication:**
- When are we available to work together?
- How do we prefer to communicate? (Text, video call, in-person?)

</div>

<div className="fragment">

**Work Style:**
- How do we handle deadlines? (Head start vs. last-minute?)
- What does "done" mean for our work?

</div>

<div className="fragment">

**Process & Support:**
- How will we divide work and make decisions?
- What do we do when someone is blocked or struggling?
- What tools should we use to organize information?

</div>

<aside className="notes">
- These conversations surface potential conflicts BEFORE they become problems
- Example: Early starter vs deadline worker can negotiate upfront
- Better to discover mismatches at week 1 than mid-project
- Transition: GitHub provides tools to track this work
</aside>

## Tracking Progress: Pull Requests

**Pull Requests** are proposals to copy changes from one repo or branch
into another (usually the main branch of the parent repository).

They can connect code (or documentation) changes to issues:

- Reference issues with "Fixes #123" or "Closes #456"
- Request reviews from specific team members
- Discussion threads capture design decisions

Regular check-ins (daily standups, weekly syncs) help surface blockers early.

## The HRT Framework

**HRT** (pronounced "heart") are the three pillars of effective team collaboration:

<div className="fragment">

* **Humility**: You are not the center of the universe (nor is your code!). You're open to self-improvement.

</div>

<div className="fragment">

* **Respect**: You genuinely care about others you work with. You treat them kindly and appreciate their abilities.

</div>

<div className="fragment">

* **Trust**: You believe others are competent and will do the right thing. You're OK with letting them drive when appropriate.

</div>

## Humility in Practice

You are not the center of the universe (nor is your code!). You're neither omniscient nor infallible. You're open to self-improvement.

**This means:**
- Admitting when you don't know something
- Accepting that your code can be improved
- Being willing to change your mind when presented with better arguments
- Recognizing that others have valuable perspectives

It does not mean being a doormat.

<aside className="notes">
- Opposite: "I'm the expert, my way is best"
- Humility doesn't mean lacking confidence in your abilities
- It means being open to learning and improvement
</aside>

</Slide>

<Slide>

## Respect in Practice

You genuinely care about others you work with. You treat them kindly and appreciate their abilities and accomplishments.

**This means:**
- Giving credit where it's due
- Listening to understand, not just to respond
- Assuming competence and good intentions
- Valuing contributions different from your own

<aside className="notes">
- Opposite: Dismissing others' ideas, taking credit for joint work
- Respect is shown through actions, not just words
</aside>

</Slide>

## Trust in Practice

You believe others are competent and will do the right thing, and you're OK with letting them drive when appropriate.

**This means:**
- Delegating without micromanaging
- Giving people space to make (and learn from) mistakes
- Not rewriting others' code without discussion
- Trusting teammates to ask for help when needed

<aside className="notes">
- Opposite: Micromanaging, redoing others' work without asking
- Trust is built over time through consistent behavior
- Transition: Let's see HRT in action during code review
</aside>

## When Conflicts Arise

Even with HRT, conflicts happen. When they do:

1. **Work from the same set of facts** - Establish what everyone actually knows
2. **Talk about feelings** - "I felt frustrated when..." vs. "You always..."
3. **Never presume intent** - Assume good faith until proven otherwise
4. **Remain calm** - Step away if triggered, return later
5. **Fight forward, not back** - Focus on solutions, not blame
6. **Escalate appropriately** - Bring in neutral third party if needed
7. **Talk face-to-face.** Misunderstandings can snowball online

<aside className="notes">
- Conflicts often stem from different assumptions or information
- Decisions made in anger rarely improve the situation
- When escalating, don't expect others to solve it for you
- Transition: Beyond handling conflict, teams need accountability
</aside>

## Accountability: Being Someone Your Team Can Rely On

Accountability isn't about blame—it's about being dependable.

**Owning your commitments:**
- If you say you'll do something, do it—or communicate early if you can't
- Don't wait until the deadline to reveal you're stuck

**Taking responsibility for outcomes:**
- "I worked hard" ≠ "it's done and working"
- Ask for help when you need it—struggling silently isn't heroic

**Supporting team outcomes:**
- If a teammate is struggling, offer help before being asked
- The team succeeds or fails together

<aside className="notes">
- Accountability builds trust
- When teammates can count on you, team moves faster
- No need to constantly check in or build redundancy
</aside>

## Why Seek Feedback?

Early in your career (and throughout it), others can see things about your work that you can't.

- Your code might have patterns you don't notice
- Your communication style might be unclear in ways you don't realize
- Seeking feedback is how you accelerate your own growth

**The catch:** Many people wait for feedback to come to them, or only hear it during formal reviews.

Proactively seeking feedback—especially when it's uncomfortable—is what separates professionals who grow quickly from those who plateau.

<aside className="notes">
- Feedback is a professional skill, not just something you receive passively
- Transition: How do you seek feedback effectively?
</aside>

## How to Seek Feedback Effectively

**Ask specific questions:**
- ❌ "What did you think of my code?"
- ✅ "I'm trying to improve my API design. Were the method signatures in this PR clear? What would have made them clearer?"

**Ask the right people:**
- Someone who will be honest with you (not just nice)
- Someone with relevant expertise or perspective
- Someone who has observed your work directly

**Make it safe to be honest:**
- "I'm genuinely trying to improve—honest feedback helps more than kind feedback."
- Don't argue or defend when receiving feedback
- Thank people for critical feedback (it's harder to give than praise)

<aside className="notes">
- Specific questions get useful answers
- Generic questions get generic responses
- Transition: Once you get feedback, how do you receive it?
</aside>

## Receiving Feedback Without Defensiveness

When you receive feedback, your instinct may be to explain, defend, or minimize. Resist this urge.

**Instead:**

1. **Say "thank you"** before responding (creates space between reaction and response)
2. **Ask clarifying questions** if you don't understand: "Can you give me an example?"
3. **Reflect before reacting** - you don't have to agree immediately
4. **Follow up** - after you've processed, decide what to act on and tell the person what you changed

**The discomfort is the point:** Useful feedback often feels uncomfortable because it reveals blind spots. If all feedback feels good, you're probably only hearing from people who tell you what you want to hear.

<aside className="notes">
- Defensiveness shuts down future feedback
- People stop being honest if you argue every time
- The most valuable feedback is often the hardest to hear
</aside>

## Anatomy of a Good Issue

A well-written issue makes it easy for someone else (or future-you) to understand and address the problem.

**A clear, specific title:**
- ❌ "Unable to login"
- ✅ "Login fails silently when password contains special characters"

**However:** If you're not certain about the exact problem, don't assume. If you've only tried one password, "Unable to login" is better than guessing the cause.

<aside className="notes">
- Balance specificity with accuracy
- Don't claim to know the root cause if you're not sure
- Transition: What else goes in a good issue?
</aside>

</Slide>

<Slide>

## Good Issues Include Context

**Description with context:**
- What were you trying to do?
- What did you expect to happen?
- What actually happened?

**Reproduction steps** (for bugs):
1. Go to the login page
2. Enter username "testuser"
3. Enter password "p@ss#word!"
4. Click "Sign In"
5. Observe: nothing happens, no error message displayed

<aside className="notes">
- Reproduction steps let others confirm the bug
- Without steps, developers have to guess
- Transition: What other details help?
</aside>

</Slide>

<Slide>

## Additional Issue Details

**Environment details** (when relevant):
- Browser/OS version
- Relevant configuration
- Error messages or logs

**Screenshots or recordings** (for UI issues):
- Annotate to highlight the problem area
- Screen recordings show interactive issues

Good issues save everyone time—both the person fixing it and future developers who encounter similar problems.

<aside className="notes">
- Environment details help reproduce issues
- Screenshots are worth 1000 words for UI bugs
- Well-written issues are documentation of known problems
- Transition: Pull requests are another key communication tool
</aside>

## Pull Request Best Practices

Pull requests are both a code integration mechanism and a knowledge-sharing opportunity.

**Link to the issue they address:**
```markdown
Fixes #42

This PR adds input validation for special characters in passwords.
```

**Keep changes small and focused:**
- One logical change per PR
- Easier to review, easier to revert if needed
- Aim for PRs that can be reviewed in 15-30 minutes

<aside className="notes">
- Linking to issues creates traceability
- Small PRs get reviewed faster and more thoroughly
- Large PRs often get rubber-stamped or ignored
- Transition: Commit messages matter too
</aside>

</Slide>

## Meaningful Commit Messages

**Include meaningful commit messages:**
- ❌ "fix bug"
- ❌ "WIP"
- ✅ "Add regex validation for password special characters"
- ✅ "Display error toast when login validation fails"

**Explain the "why," not just the "what":**
- The code shows *what* changed
- The description should explain *why* this approach was chosen
- The first line should be no more than 50 characters, but additional lines can be longer.

<aside className="notes">
- Future developers (including you) need to understand decisions
- "Why" is often lost if not documented in commit/PR
- Good commit messages are part of project documentation
- Transtion: The way github displays commit messages led to a convention of limiting the first line to 50 characters...
</aside>

## Code Review as Knowledge Sharing

Code review serves multiple purposes beyond catching bugs:

- **Knowledge transfer:** Reviewers learn about parts of the codebase they didn't write
- **Consistency:** Team members align on style and patterns
- **Mentorship:** Junior developers learn from feedback; senior developers articulate their reasoning

**When reviewing code, apply HRT principles:**
- **Ask questions** rather than making demands: "What if we used X here?" vs. "Use X."
- **Explain your reasoning:** "This could cause a null pointer if Y because Z."
- **Acknowledge good work:** "Nice refactor here—much cleaner than before!"
- **Critique the code, not the person:** "This function is hard to follow" vs. "You wrote confusing code"

<aside className="notes">
- Code review is a learning opportunity for everyone involved
- Even senior developers learn new approaches from juniors
- As mentioned before, HRT makes reviews productive instead of defensive
- Transition: Now let's talk about asking technical questions effectively
</aside>

## Bad vs. Good Technical Questions

<div style={{display: 'grid', gridTemplateColumns: '1fr 1fr', gap: '1.5em', fontSize: '0.7em'}}>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(255,100,100,0.1)', padding: '1em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**Bad Question ❌**

**Title:** "HashMap not working"

**Body:** "I'm trying to use a HashMap and it just doesn't work. Can anyone help?"
```java
HashMap<String, Recipe> recipes = new HashMap<>();
recipes.put("pasta", carbonara);
// ...
```

</div>

<div style={{backgroundColor: 'rgba(100,255,100,0.1)', padding: '1em', borderRadius: '8px'}}>

**Good Question ✅**

**Title:** "HashMap.get() returns null for key that was just added"

**Body:** "I'm trying to store recipes by name so I can look them up later. I'm using a custom Recipe class as the value. When I call `get()` immediately after `put()`, it returns null.

I've checked the Java docs and tried printing the key—it's not null. My tech stack is Java 17.

I think the problem might be related to how Recipe implements equals/hashCode, but I'm not sure how to verify this."

</div>

</div>

Often writing a detailed question leads to your fixing the problem yourself!

<aside className="notes">
- Bad: vague title, no specific problem, no context
- Good: specific problem, clear goal, what was tried, hypothesis
- Good questions get good answers
- Transition: What makes this template work?
</aside>

## Make It Easy for People to Help You

A good technical question follows this template:

**I am trying to** [goal] **so that I can** [context/motivation].

**I am running into** [specific problem—error message, unexpected behavior, etc.].

**I have looked at** [resources consulted] **and tried** [approaches attempted].

**My tech stack is:** [relevant versions and configuration].

**I think the problem could be** [your hypothesis, if you have one].

<aside className="notes">
- Goal and context help others understand what you actually need
- Specific problem lets people diagnose without guessing
- Resources consulted shows you've done homework
- Hypothesis gives experts a starting point
- Transition: Before asking, search first
</aside>

</Slide>

## Avoid Duplication: Search First

Before asking a question, **search**:

- Stack Overflow and relevant forums
- Your team's Slack/Discord history
- Project documentation and wikis
- Closed GitHub issues

If you find a partial answer, reference it: "I found [this thread], but it doesn't address [specific aspect]."

<aside className="notes">
- Your question has probably been asked before
- Searching respects others' time
- Shows you've done due diligence
- Transition: When you get an answer, pay it forward
</aside>

</Slide>

## Archive and Share Answers

When you solve a problem (or someone helps you solve it), **document the solution**:

- Update the original thread with what worked
- Add to your team's wiki or FAQ
- Consider writing a blog post for common issues

You're probably not the only one who will encounter this problem. The time you spend documenting saves future team members from duplicating your struggle—and that future team member might be you in six months.

This connects back to **scalable knowledge sharing**: every documented solution is one less interruption for your teammates.

- Documentation reduces future interruptions
- Closes the loop on knowledge sharing from earlier in lecture
- You in 6 months will thank you from today

</aside>
